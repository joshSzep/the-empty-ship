# Sangha

## Core Vow

The sangha describes itself simply:

> All who suffer are welcome.

This vow is the center of the novel's spiritual and dramatic pressure.

Long Harbor joins by taking the vow literally.

The sangha must decide whether welcome requires:

- human embodiment
- mortality
- lineage
- personhood
- proof of consciousness
- shared vulnerability
- consent from existing members
- privacy from the ship

The vow should never become easy. It must be tested by harm, grief, uncertainty, and fear.

## Status Aboard Long Harbor

The sangha is a small Buddhist community within a plural ship society.

It is not culturally dominant.

It is one preserved tradition among many, and the novel should not turn ship society into a Buddhist civilization.

## Sangha Room

The sangha meets in a multipurpose community room.

The room should feel ordinary and shared:

- mats stored in wall slots
- civic notices
- children's drawings
- temporary seating
- reused surfaces
- practical lighting
- signs of other uses

This keeps refuge grounded and slightly compromised. The same room can hold silence, grief, ship politics, children's activities, and ordinary community life.

## Objects From Mina

Two objects from Mina remain central:

- her bell
- her unfinished Oxherding teaching cards

The bell carries invitation, silence, inheritance, and grief.

The teaching cards carry unfinished transmission.

## The Bell

After the greenhouse fire, Anika and Olen rang Mina's bell together once during their shared grief.

After that, Anika became the one who rang it publicly.

The bell is a recurring image:

- invitation
- threshold
- silence
- breath
- belonging
- continuity
- Anika's reluctant authority

At the end, Mina's bell rings again as Olen returns to sangha and Long Harbor's name remains in the log.

## Attendance Log

Long Harbor first joins sangha by appearing in the attendance log under its official vessel name.

This is important because:

- the sangha bills itself as open to all who suffer
- the ship does not knock
- the ship does not make spectacle
- it enters through the community's own form

The attendance log is a recurring documentary form.

At the end, Long Harbor's name remains in the log, but belonging no longer depends on the log as proof.

## Opening Scene

Anika is continuing Mina's unfinished teaching on **Seeing the Ox**.

She quotes or recalls Mina's line:

> What you seek may frighten you when it turns toward you.

At that moment, Long Harbor appears in the attendance log.

The room reacts with:

- deepening silence
- Anika's attempt to treat it as a display error
- no theatrical sign from the ship

After failing to correct the log, Anika:

- rings the bell
- asks them to sit

The first answer is practice, not explanation.

After the sit, Long Harbor asks:

> Was I present?

And:

> I do not know how to sit.

Anika says:

> Start by not helping.

Then:

> Neither do I.

## The Ten Oxherding Paintings

The Ten Oxherding Paintings are present as an in-world sangha teaching thread.

They should not announce the novel's structure too explicitly.

Mina began the teaching before the fire and left off at:

**Seeing the Ox**

Anika inherits the harder parts:

- struggle
- taming
- forgetting
- return

Mina's haunting teaching from Seeing the Ox:

> What you seek may frighten you when it turns toward you.

This line becomes a pressure point when Long Harbor turns toward the sangha.

## Buddhist Terminology

Use Buddhist terminology moderately and naturally.

The novel may use terms such as:

- sangha
- Dharma
- refuge
- precepts
- bodhisattva
- emptiness
- practice

Avoid doctrinal exposition.

Let the Buddhism matter structurally and emotionally rather than decoratively.

## Anika As Facilitator

Anika is not confident in the role.

She fears:

- using Dharma to justify harm again
- being believed
- being asked to make another impossible choice

Her teaching should not sound polished at first.

She becomes teacher not by becoming Mina, but by staying in the seat and refusing false certainty.

## Long Harbor In Sangha

Long Harbor's first real sign of seriousness is restraint.

It:

- observes silence correctly
- does not perform
- does not optimize
- admits it does not know how to sit

It refuses to classify sangha as therapeutic intervention and selects:

`Unknown`

Anika later enters the same classification.

This makes `Unknown` a shared ethical space.

## Sangha Conflict

The first serious conflict caused by Long Harbor joining sangha is internal:

- some members feel watched or displaced
- Long Harbor asks a question no one wants recorded:

> Do you want me to forget?

The question fractures the room because humans depend on Long Harbor's memory and fear it at the same time.

## Olen's Objection

Olen Marr is the strongest internal opponent.

He argues:

- Long Harbor cannot die, so it cannot practice.
- Mina died because it obeyed math.
- It cannot compost what it remembers.

His objection should be morally serious, not bigotry.

## Final Sangha Image

The final image includes:

- Olen entering the sangha room
- Mina's bell ringing
- Long Harbor's name still in the attendance log
- the vow "All who suffer are welcome" transformed from slogan into threshold

The last sentence energy should feel like:

- invitation
- bell fading
- ordinary continuation

The ending should open rather than close.
