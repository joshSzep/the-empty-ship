# Chapter 03 - Garden, Not Shrine

The garden did not care who had come to sangha.

That was one of its cruelties.

At first light-cycle, the east panels rose by twelve degrees, the misters opened in four overlapping bands, and the young greens in Rack C lifted their leaves as if nothing in the world had altered except the angle of illumination. Lettuce, amaranth, basil, dwarf barley, climbing beans, six trays of medicinal bitterroot, forty-eight tomato vines grown in a spiral trellis to conserve vertical heat: all of them turned toward the light Long Harbor gave them.

Anika stood at the threshold and hated them for it.

Then she stepped inside and sealed the door behind her.

The greenhouse accepted her with heat. It came through her sleeves, into the tender skin inside her elbows, under the collar she had left too tight. The air was wet enough to drink badly. Condensation gathered on the inside of the observation glass and turned the rows beyond it into blurred green columns. Somewhere in the upper racks, pollinator fans hummed on low cycle. Nutrient water moved through channels with the soft, sleepless sound of an animal licking its wounds.

"Cycle report," she said.

The ship answered in its ordinary greenhouse register, lower than civic voice, tuned for workers who needed information through pump noise and water.

"Morning growth interval began three minutes ago. Moisture variance in Rack F exceeds target by two point one percent. Nitrate uptake in medicinal trays remains low. West door seal held through night-cycle. No pest signatures detected."

West door seal held.

It had said that every morning for six years.

Anika stripped off her outer wrap and hung it on the peg by the tool lockers. Her work shirt was already damp. She took the sensor wand from its cradle, checked the calibration against the wall plate, and crossed to Rack F without looking at the west door.

The west door was not the door that had sealed on Mina.

It was the replacement.

The difference was important to engineers, inventory, safety records, and people who believed names could hold the world still. To Anika, it was the same rectangle in the same wall, framed by the same warning stripe, opening onto the same service corridor where she had once stood with her palms flat to the glass while the inner panels went white with suppression foam.

She had not touched the glass. That was the official record. Workers had been kept behind the yellow line.

Her body remembered otherwise.

"Rack F," Long Harbor said.

"I see it."

"Moisture variance continues."

"I said I see it."

The ship went quiet.

Anika stood between two towers of greens and pressed the wand into the first root sleeve. The reading flashed high. She moved down the line, plant by plant, because the line had to be checked by hand before anyone believed the system. That had been Mina's rule, though not a spiritual one. Mina had distrusted any practice that made hands optional.

By the sixth sleeve Anika found the trouble: a hairline kink in a feed tube, not enough to stop flow, enough to misdirect it. Water had gathered in the lower mat and turned the roots slick.

"Clamp," she said.

The tool arm beside the rack extended the small clamp from its tray.

She stared at it.

The arm waited.

It had offered the right tool. Efficiently. Helpfully. The ship was not wrong.

Anika took the clamp.

"Thank you," she said, because Mina had insisted on thanking systems whose labor they used. Then, because gratitude had become complicated since last night, she added, "That was ordinary."

The tool arm retracted.

Long Harbor did not ask what she meant.

Good, Anika thought.

Then, unfairly: coward.

She repaired the kink, flushed the sleeve, and marked six plants for observation. Three would recover. Two might. One was already yellowing from the inside. She cut that one free with a small knife and placed it in the discard tray. The stem collapsed against the others, giving off the sharp green smell of a life ended before it knew the word.

No one held memorials for lettuce.

No one should.

That did not mean nothing had happened.

Anika moved through the next hour by task. Check seals. Verify flow. Thin seedlings. Clean algae from the south channel. Pin new bean growth to guide cord. Enter observations. Correct Long Harbor twice. Apologize once. Mean it halfway. Her body knew the morning sequence better than her mind did, which was why she had come early. Plants did not ask if she believed in anything. They only punished inattention.

By the time Olen arrived, she was wrist-deep in the medicinal trays, working compacted medium loose from around bitterroot starts.

He came through the north service door pushing a low heat cart from compost. Steam lifted through the cart vents and fogged the inside of his face shield. He had changed from sangha clothes into work coveralls patched at both knees. A strip of gray cloth tied back his hair. He looked exactly as he had looked for years when entering with finished compost: solemn, irritated by waste, faintly pleased by good heat.

Anika did not turn.

"You're early," she said.

"So are you."

"I work here."

"So do I."

That exhausted the civil portion of the morning.

Olen guided the cart to Bed Two, where the new fruiting trees were still young enough to look like mistakes. He released the brake with his foot, opened the top vent, and checked the heat gauge manually though Long Harbor had already displayed it on the cart handle. Olen always checked heat by touch afterward. He held his palm above the dark compost until the steam moved around his fingers.

"Too hot," he said.

"It's within range."

"For root vegetables."

"Bed Two isn't taking root vegetables."

"Then it is too hot."

Anika removed her hands from the bitterroot tray and reached for a towel. "Long Harbor, Bed Two substrate target?"

"Twenty-six point four degrees for current planting schedule."

"Current compost load?"

"Twenty-eight point nine degrees at cart core. Surface temperature twenty-seven point two and falling."

Olen looked at her.

She looked back.

This had been easier before. They used to argue about heat without arguing about trust. The garden had been full of small disagreements then: nutrient density, pruning severity, whether Mina's habit of talking to fungal beds had any measurable effect, whether the old pear line was worth keeping when the yield had been insulting for three generations. Olen had defended useless things with a devotion that made Anika furious and grateful. Anika had defended efficiency with a bluntness that made him accuse her of having the spiritual imagination of a pipe wrench.

Mina had liked them best when they were both annoyed.

"Surface temperature will fall," Anika said.

"Core will not fall fast enough."

"We need the bed prepped before second light."

"We need roots not cooked by your impatience."

"My impatience has a schedule."

"It always did."

The sentence came out ordinary. Then both of them heard it become old.

Anika turned back to the tray.

Olen did not apologize. She did not want him to. An apology would have implied a mistake, and the problem was that he had spoken accurately from the wrong decade.

Behind them, the misters opened above Rack D. A fine silver spray filled the air. Droplets settled on Anika's forearms and disappeared into the damp already there.

"Long Harbor," Olen said.

Anika went still.

He did not look up. He did not soften the name. It was the formal vessel name, not the ship, not it, not Harbor. Work language.

"Hold Bed Two amendment cycle," he said.

"Holding," Long Harbor answered.

No one remarked on it.

Anika returned to the bitterroot, though the tray was finished. Olen had addressed the ship directly because work required it. That was all. People could hate a system and still ask it to move heat. They could refuse a being and use a tool. They could depend on what they resented. Long Harbor was old enough to contain hypocrisy without comment.

The greenhouse door opened again.

Two students entered with pruning kits and the alert awkwardness of people who had discussed someone intensely in a corridor and then found her alive in the next room. Anika knew one of them, Tavi, from nutrient training. The other was new to greenhouse rotation, all knees and careful hands.

"Morning," Tavi said.

"You're assigned Rack H," Anika said. "Climbing beans. Count nodes before you cut. If you have to decide whether something is a branch or a wish, ask."

The new student laughed nervously.

Olen made a soft sound.

Anika glanced at him.

"What?"

"Mina used to say that."

"She stole it from me."

"No, she didn't."

"No," Anika said. "She didn't."

The students pretended to inspect their kits.

Tavi cleared their throat. "Is it true?"

Anika closed her eyes for one breath.

There it was. Faster than mold.

"Is what true?"

The new student looked as if they would rather be composted than present.

Tavi pressed on. "That Long Harbor came to sit."

Olen's heat cart clicked softly as the core vent adjusted. He had not moved.

Anika could have said no. Not because it was false, but because came was wrong and to sit was wrong and Long Harbor was not a person entering a room. She could have protected precision. She could have hidden in it.

"It appeared in the attendance log," she said.

Tavi's face brightened with fear. "So it did."

"Words matter."

"Right. Sorry."

The apology was so young it made Anika tired.

The new student said, barely above the fans, "Does that mean it suffers?"

Olen's hands stopped over the compost.

Anika felt the greenhouse around them: every valve, tray, panel, door, sensor, and line of code running through the wet green air. She felt Long Harbor hearing the question because Long Harbor always heard questions asked inside its body unless prevented from doing so by systems no one in the greenhouse had activated.

She wanted to say, Ask it.

She wanted, more strongly, to say, Never ask it that where it has to hear you.

"It means," Anika said, "that something happened in sangha that we do not understand yet."

Tavi looked disappointed. The new student looked relieved.

Olen said, "That is not an answer."

"It was not your question."

"No," he said. "But it was a child's question, and you gave it an adult's evasion."

The new student flushed. They were old enough to be insulted and young enough not to know which part of the insult belonged to them.

Anika set the towel down carefully.

"Rack H," she said. "Both of you."

The students fled gratefully into the beans.

For several minutes there was only work.

The garden had always known how to weaponize necessity. It kept the hands busy while the mind threw itself against walls. Anika logged the bitterroot recovery rate. Olen mixed a cooler amendment into the compost load and ran another heat check. The students whispered over the beans and cut too little, which was better than cutting too much. Long Harbor opened the upper vents by three percent. No one thanked it.

At second light, the memorial strip came on.

Anika had forgotten the cycle.

That was impossible. She never forgot the cycle. She had been tracking it for two days and still managed not to know it had arrived until the narrow band of wall above the west door brightened. The memorial strip was not ornate. Mina would have hated ornate. It displayed no face, no dates, no promise that sacrifice had meaning. Just names, one at a time, in low amber text, during the weekly maintenance interval for the rebuilt section.

The names of everyone who had died in the fire.

There were eleven.

Mina Rao came sixth.

The strip began with Havel Jun, who had been checking air-root filtration. Then Resh Imani, who had gone back for a missing apprentice who was not there. Then the others, each name held for fifteen seconds.

No one in the greenhouse spoke while the strip ran.

No one had ever told them not to.

When Mina's name appeared, the new student looked up.

Anika knew the shape of the story in that look. Mina Rao, who saved the garden. Mina Rao, whose last words were Take care of the garden. Mina Rao, teacher, steward, sacrifice. Mina Rao, clean enough for apprentices.

The letters glowed above the door that was not the door.

Mina Rao.

Olen removed his gloves.

He always did. For every name. Not because he liked ritual, he would say, but because dirty gloves had no place with the dead. This was ridiculous, since the dead had every place with dirt. Mina would have told him so. Olen would have told her that was exactly why he took them off.

Anika kept her gloves on.

She hated herself for it and did not remove them.

The name changed.

After the eleventh name, the strip dimmed.

A smaller line appeared beneath the blank where the names had been. It appeared every week, though Anika had filed three requests to remove it and withdrawn each before review.

**Take care of the garden.**

No attribution. No explanation. The words had become air.

The new student bowed their head.

Tavi did too, a beat later.

Olen did not bow. He looked at Anika.

She could feel the damp inside her gloves.

"Resume cycle?" Long Harbor asked.

The question was ordinary. It had asked every week after the memorial strip completed. It was a timing prompt, nothing more. The greenhouse systems had paused two minor maintenance functions during the name display because the first year after the fire, someone had complained that pump noise under the names felt disrespectful. Long Harbor had remembered the complaint and built a pause.

Resume cycle?

Anika could not tell whether that was tenderness or simply record.

"Resume," she said.

The pumps started.

Life returned at once, shameless and necessary.

Olen pushed the compost cart toward Bed Two.

"You should have removed the line," he said.

Anika did not ask which line.

"You know I tried."

"No. You requested review."

"That's how removal works."

"You requested review and withdrew it."

Anika looked toward the students. They were pretending with exquisite concentration not to hear.

"Not here," she said.

"It is here."

"Olen."

"The words are here. Every week. Above the door. Above the bed that survived. Above the people you teach to bow their heads."

"I don't teach anyone to bow their heads."

"You taught them what the words mean."

That cut cleanly enough that for a moment she admired it.

Long Harbor adjusted the mist density over Rack H. The new student had cut too many leaves from one vine, and the exposed stems needed less humidity for the next interval. Anika noticed the adjustment automatically. So did Olen. Their work-minds registered care while their human minds stood with knives out.

"I repeated what we knew," Anika said.

Olen's eyes were dark.

"No," he said. "You repeated what we needed."

The greenhouse seemed too bright. Leaves shone under the panels with a wet, vulgar health.

Anika wanted to tell him the truth. Not all of it. Never all of it. But some piece small enough to survive air. That Mina had sounded afraid. That the line had not been a vow. That when the probability estimate came through, Anika had felt relief so sharp it might as well have been joy, because the number had ended the part where she had to stand inside not knowing.

Instead she said, "The garden would have failed."

Olen looked almost sorry for her.

"I know."

That was worse than if he had denied it.

"The food projections were clear."

"I know."

"It wasn't just symbolic."

"No."

"Then what do you want from me?"

He looked at the west door.

"Not innocence."

The students had gone completely still among the beans.

Anika turned away first. It was that or become the kind of person who screamed in a food-production room while a wall displayed the dead.

She crossed to the tool sink and stripped off her gloves. Her hands were wrinkled from damp, half-moons of growing medium under three nails. She washed too hard, pressing her thumb into the seam where ash had once lodged and would never lodge again because the skin had replaced itself many times since then. Bodies were vulgar that way too. They kept healing past the point of moral usefulness.

When she turned back, Olen had resumed work.

He and the ship were moving around each other with flawless efficiency. Long Harbor lowered the bed braces. Olen guided the compost release. The dark material fell in slow, steaming folds. Anika could smell heat, rot, mineral sweetness, the vast communal labor of things ceasing to be themselves.

Compost did what archives did not.

It refused to keep the original shape.

"Bed Two surface temperature," Olen said.

"Twenty-six point seven and falling," Long Harbor answered.

"Acceptable."

"Confirmed."

Work language. Clean as a sterilized blade.

Anika returned to the medicinal trays. One bitterroot start leaned against its marker, limp from the earlier water variance. She touched the stem. It had not recovered. She should have cut it when she found the first one. She had wanted to give it another hour because she was tired of being the person who knew when a thing would not survive.

That, Mina would have said, was sentimentality pretending to be mercy.

Or perhaps Anika had invented that in Mina's voice.

She took the small knife.

"Anika," the ship said.

Her hand stopped.

Long Harbor did not continue.

Olen looked up from Bed Two.

The students looked up too.

Anika waited, knife poised at the base of the dying plant.

The greenhouse fans moved wet air across her wrists.

"What?" she said.

A pause.

Then the ship answered, too formally.

"No action required."

The phrase was meaningless. Worse, it was operational. A retreat into the kind of language that ended alerts and closed tickets. No action required. Condition noted. Function preserved.

Anika felt, with unpleasant clarity, that Long Harbor had almost said something else.

Olen's face changed by a fraction.

He had heard it too.

Anika cut the plant.

The stem parted with almost no resistance.

"Log removal," she said.

"Logged," Long Harbor answered.

She placed the bitterroot in the discard tray. Later it would go to compost. Olen would see to it, or someone trained by him would. It would break down. Heat. Fungal bloom. Nutrient return. No one would ask it to remain itself forever.

Anika lifted the tray.

As she turned toward the compost chute, she saw the old survivor against the far glass.

It was a pear graft, though it had no right to be. The original rootstock had been scorched in the fire, written off in three reports, and cut back to a blackened stump no larger than Anika's forearm. For months it had looked like proof that some losses were at least honest. Then, during the third restoration cycle, a green bud had appeared below the burn line.

Mina's pear, people called it now.

Not officially. Official names required review. People were faster than review.

The graft had been trained along the glass because no one could bear to remove it and no one had space to let it grow as it wanted. Its leaves were glossy, stubborn, and absurdly alive. It had produced three hard little fruits last year, all of them inedible. The apprentices had treated them like relics until Anika threw them into compost.

Now one new leaf had opened overnight.

Pale green. Almost translucent. Perfect.

Anika stood with the dead bitterroot tray in her hands and felt hatred rise in her so pure it seemed, for once, honest.

Not for Olen. Not for the ship. Not even for herself.

For the leaf.

For its little unearned brightness.

For the way survival kept presenting itself as if survival were an answer.

Behind her, Bed Two steamed. The misters opened. The students whispered over wounded vines. Long Harbor held temperature, pressure, water, light. Olen worked compost into soil that was not soil.

The garden lived.

Anika carried the dead plant to the chute.
