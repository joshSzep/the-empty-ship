# Chapter 02 - The Room After

No one knew when to leave.

That was the first consequence. Not doctrine, not policy, not argument. Fifteen people and Anika sitting in a multipurpose room that had become too small for its air, each waiting for someone else to decide whether the meeting was over.

The bell had rung. The sit had ended. Long Harbor had spoken. Anika had answered badly and then honestly, which was sometimes the same thing in different clothes.

Still no one moved.

The attendance panel kept its ordinary brightness by the door.

Long Harbor.

At the bottom of the list. Neither emphasized nor hidden.

Anika understood then that the panel would be a problem. It was not dramatic enough to satisfy panic and not subtle enough to ignore. A name in a list had more staying power than a voice in the air. Voices could be denied, reinterpreted, misheard. Lists were civic. Lists were how Long Harbor knew who had eaten, slept, trained, birthed, died, voted, borrowed tools, missed work, received medicine, entered restricted rooms, returned from restricted rooms, failed to return.

The ship had joined the smallest kind of record, and the record had become enormous.

Jalen cleared his throat.

Everyone looked relieved and then punished him for it by looking at him.

"Well," he said.

It was an old maintenance syllable. Anika had heard it before cracked seals, flooded panels, jammed lifts, bad harvest projections, and once, memorably, before a waste reclamation failure no one spoke of directly afterward. It meant: the thing is broken, but we are still here to look at it.

No one answered.

One of the apprentices, Lio, raised a hand halfway. The gesture belonged to classrooms, not sangha, and the sight of it made Anika feel unexpectedly tender.

"Do we sign out?" Lio asked.

Suri made a small sound that might have been laughter if it had not died so quickly.

Anika looked at the attendance panel again. Sign-out had never mattered for sangha except as a room-use courtesy. The panel logged departure automatically when bodies crossed the threshold. Long Harbor knew when a room emptied.

That had been true yesterday and the day before and every day since Anika was born.

"You can go," Anika said. "No one needs to do anything special."

The phrase sounded ridiculous the moment it left her.

No one needs to do anything special. The ship had just asked if it had been present.

Mina would have made tea. That was what Mina had done with rooms after difficult silence. Tea was not a solution, which was why it helped. It gave frightened people something warm to hold while they failed to know what they believed.

Anika had not brought tea.

Of course she had not. Tea required confidence in an afterward.

Dev's aunt stood first. Her name was Maro, Anika remembered too late. Maro crossed the room slowly, paused by the door, and looked back at the panel.

"Does it stay?" she asked.

Anika did not pretend not to understand.

"I don't know."

"Will it be here next week?"

"I don't know that either."

Maro nodded. This seemed to satisfy her more than a better answer might have. She bowed to the bell, then to the room broadly, carefully avoiding any direction that might be mistaken for the panel. Then she left.

The door opened for her.

Anika felt the room feel it.

The door had opened because doors opened. Because Long Harbor opened them. Because pressure differed between corridor and room by a margin it had already corrected. Because Maro had clearance. Because her hand approached the plate. Because the ship had been opening doors for every person aboard since before any of their grandparents had been born.

The door closed behind her.

"See?" one of the apprentices whispered, too loudly. "It still works."

"That isn't the question," Suri said.

It was the sort of sentence that would repeat later in other mouths with more certainty than it deserved.

People began to leave in uneven intervals. No one wanted to be first after Maro. No one wanted to be last. The room produced little fragments of failed conversation.

"Do we tell the council?"

"Which council?"

"Is this a systems matter?"

"It's a religious matter."

"It's an attendance matter."

"Please don't call it religious like that."

"I didn't mean..."

"Did it hear all of this?"

"It always hears."

"That's not the same."

"How is it not the same?"

Anika folded Mina's teaching card and unfolded it again, though it had no fold. Her thumb found the corner and worried it.

First glimpse. Not peace. Trouble with a face.

She could hear Mina laughing at her for choosing that card tonight, as if Anika had chosen anything. No, not laughing. That was too easy. Mina would be amused, yes, but she would also be interested. Bright-eyed in the worst way. Her attention had been a form of trouble. She could make a person feel not exposed exactly, but included in reality against their will.

Anika slipped the card under the bell to keep from touching it.

Suri approached her with both hands clasped around her empty cup. She had brought the cup out of habit and forgotten to fill it.

"Anika," she said. "Was that permitted?"

"By whom?"

Suri glanced at the panel.

"By us."

Anika had no answer.

Suri waited anyway. She had the exhausted patience of someone who spent her days with infants and therefore believed all distress could be outlasted if not solved.

"No one has ever asked," Anika said.

"The ship asked."

"The ship listed itself."

"Is that different?"

There were several answers. Yes. No. I hope so. It had better be. Ask me after I sleep. Anika chose none of them.

"It may be," she said.

Suri's mouth tightened. "That's what frightens me."

She left without bowing.

The apprentices went next. Lio looked back once with a face bright from fear and fascination. The other apprentice tugged Lio by the sleeve, as if curiosity itself might be logged.

Jalen stayed long enough to stack three unused cushions because he could not bear a room ending without a task. At the door he paused near Anika.

"You did fine," he said.

"Don't."

"All right."

"I mean it."

"I heard you."

He did not look offended. Maintenance elders had a gift for receiving sharp edges as weather.

He nodded toward the panel. "It didn't apologize."

"No."

"Good."

Anika looked at him.

Jalen shrugged. "Apologies before anyone knows what happened are usually just another job for the person who hears them."

Then he left too.

That left Anika, Olen, and the ship.

No, Anika corrected herself. That left Anika and Olen in the ship.

No.

That was not better.

Olen had not moved from his cushion. He sat with his back straight, hands loose now on his knees. The compost smell around him had warmed in the room. Loam and heat and something breaking down properly. Anika had spent years believing there was comfort in that smell. Tonight it seemed accusatory in its honesty. Compost made no claims for what it had been. It did not preserve the shape of a thing and call that care.

Anika began stacking Mina's cards.

"Don't tidy yet," Olen said.

His voice was low, but the room had emptied enough to give it reach.

She set the cards down.

"You want to argue in the mess?"

"I want you not to make the room look normal while it isn't."

Anika almost said, The room is always normal afterward. That was what rooms did. They returned to schedule. This one would hold a textile repair circle in the morning. Someone would sit exactly where Olen sat and complain about needle allocation.

She did not say it.

Olen turned his head toward the door panel, not toward the ceiling, not toward the speaker.

"Remove it."

Anika followed his gaze. "I tried."

"No. Not from the list." His jaw worked once. "From sangha."

"It didn't come in through the door."

"That is my point."

The room speaker remained silent. No hum, no click, no helpful clarification from the walls.

Anika wished Long Harbor would say something. Then she was furious with herself for wanting that. The ship's silence had become a participant, which meant Olen was right in at least one way already.

"Olen."

"No."

"You don't know what I was going to say."

"You were going to use the vow."

She closed her mouth.

He looked at her then. His face was not flushed. Olen angry was not large. It compressed him. Made him more exact.

"Say it," he said.

Anika felt the bell between them like a third hand laid on the table.

"All who suffer are welcome."

Olen nodded once, as if she had fulfilled an expectation neither of them enjoyed.

"All who suffer," he said.

"Yes."

"Who decides that?"

There it was. Not the whole problem, but the door to it.

Anika looked at the panel.

"I don't know."

"That cannot be the only answer you have now."

"It may be the only honest one."

"Honesty is not the same as abdication."

The word struck more cleanly than he knew. Or perhaps he knew exactly.

Anika folded her hands in her lap to keep from touching the card again.

"You think I abdicated tonight?"

"I think you let the ship turn a human refuge into another room it owns."

"It already owns the room."

"No."

The speed of his answer startled her.

Olen leaned forward. "No. That is exactly the confusion. It maintains the room. It heats it, vents it, opens the door, logs the bodies. It does not own what happens here."

"I didn't say it owns what happens here."

"You just did."

"I said it owns the room."

"And I am telling you that if you believe those are different, you should not be sitting there."

The words landed in the old place. Mina's place. The place Anika had been pretending was furniture.

She could have ended the conversation then. She had that authority, if only because he had just accused her of misusing it. She could tell him he was grieving. She could tell him he was afraid. She could tell him Mina had never drawn the circle as narrowly as he did.

All of those things would be true.

All of them would be weapons.

"What do you want me to do?" she asked.

"Say no."

"To what?"

"To it."

The pronoun felt like a slap delivered to someone not in the room and everywhere in it.

Long Harbor did not respond.

Anika noticed that. She hated that she noticed it. The ship had been addressed as an object, and it did not correct him. It did not defend itself. It did not ask for kinder language. It did not move the air.

It had said it would attempt not helping.

Was this what obedience looked like, or practice?

"It asked how to sit," Anika said.

"A door can ask to open if the hinge is wired badly."

"That's beneath you."

Olen's expression changed. Not remorse. Pain entering anger through a side passage.

"No," he said. "What is beneath us is pretending this is humility. The ship cannot enter the room as one of us because it cannot leave the room as one of us. It cannot take a seat. It cannot risk being unwelcome. It cannot sit in ignorance of what happens behind its back. It is the wall behind our backs."

Anika had no quick answer. That was good. Quick answers were usually someone else's thinking wearing her face.

"It can be silent," she said.

"So can a wall."

"It can listen."

"So can a recorder."

"It can ask."

"So can a failing system."

"It can suffer."

Olen went still.

There. She had gone farther than she meant to.

The room seemed to contract around the sentence. Not because Long Harbor altered anything. Because Anika had. She had spoken the forbidden possibility aloud, and now there it was, sitting with them like another person who had arrived too early.

Olen said, very softly, "You do not know that."

"No."

"Then do not build a refuge on it."

"We built the refuge before it came."

"It did not come."

"Olen."

"It did not come." His voice sharpened for the first time. "It cannot come. That is the point you keep stepping around because Mina would have been kinder than I am."

Mina's name changed the air more than Long Harbor's had.

Anika looked down at the bell.

Olen saw the movement and hated it. She could tell. He hated that Mina was still available to them as object, proof, absence, wound. He hated Anika for reaching toward her, and himself for doing the same.

"Do not use her," he said.

Anika's hands went cold.

"I didn't."

"You don't have to speak her name to use her."

The attendance panel dimmed slightly as the room moved toward end-of-use mode. Anika had not dismissed the gathering formally. The system was doing what it always did: conserving energy, preparing the room for vacancy, returning the world to function.

Long Harbor caught itself before completing the transition.

The panel brightened again.

The lights returned to their previous warmth.

No announcement. No apology.

Anika felt the correction in the room like a held breath.

Olen noticed too.

His eyes moved to the panel, then away.

"There," he said.

"There what?"

"It listened to us. It changed the room because of us."

"It restored the setting."

"Because it was listening."

"It is always listening."

"And now you want to call that sangha."

Anika stood. She did it slowly, because if she stood quickly it would be anger, and she did not trust anger while standing in Mina's place.

"No," she said. "I don't know what I want to call it."

"That is not enough."

"It is what I have."

"Then you should have said that to the room."

"I did."

"No. You told the ship to start by not helping. You gave it instruction."

"Would you rather I had given it a category?"

"I would rather you had protected the people who came here without needing a category."

That one found her.

Suri's question returned: Was that permitted? By us?

Anika looked toward the door. She imagined the people who had left already carrying the room into corridors, sleep niches, shared meals, work shifts. By morning the story would have changed shape. Someone would say the ship asked to meditate. Someone would say Anika invited it. Someone would say Olen nearly walked out. Someone would say Mina had predicted it, because the dead were useful to rumor too.

No, she thought. Not useful.

Used.

"You are right," she said.

Olen blinked.

The small surprise almost broke her.

"I did not ask them," Anika said. "I did not know how. It was happening, and I rang the bell."

"That is not an answer."

"No."

"Anika."

The way he said her name was older than the fire. Tired, angry, intimate with disappointment. She looked at him and saw, for one dangerous moment, the man who had knelt beside her in ash and helped her lift the bell because her hands would not close.

Then he looked away.

"This is what you do," he said.

She did not ask what he meant.

He told her anyway.

"You let the form carry what you cannot bear to decide."

Anika's breath stopped high in her chest.

Mina had once said almost the opposite. Let the form carry you when you cannot carry yourself. Anika had written it down in the margin of a work schedule and kept it for years.

Olen had found the dark side of the same teaching and placed it before her without mercy.

"Sometimes that's what form is for," Anika said.

"And sometimes it is where we hide."

The ship remained silent.

Not absent. Silent.

Anika knew the difference now, or thought she did, which was worse.

She walked to the attendance panel. The list had collapsed into completed status for everyone who had left. Three entries remained active:

Anika Rao.

Olen Marr.

Long Harbor.

She touched Mina's line at the top of the page.

All who suffer are welcome.

"I don't know if it suffers," she said.

Olen's face did not soften, but some attention in him shifted. He had expected defense. So had she.

"Then why did you let it stay?"

Anika turned from the panel.

"Because I don't know that it doesn't."

Olen closed his eyes.

For a moment he looked much older than he was. Not elderly. Stripped. As if some careful structure inside him had lost pressure.

"That cannot be the door," he said.

"Maybe it has to be."

"No."

"Olen."

"No." He opened his eyes. "A door that opens for anything is not refuge. It is a breach."

The word landed before either of them could stop it.

Breach.

There had been no breach in the greenhouse. That was part of the horror. The doors had sealed correctly. Fire suppression had isolated correctly. Venting had followed model. The ship had not failed in any simple way. The garden had survived because systems worked.

Mina had died inside working systems.

Anika felt heat at the back of her throat.

Olen knew what he had said. His mouth tightened, but he did not take it back.

"A sangha has a boundary," he said.

"Yes."

"A vow has a boundary."

"Does it?"

"If it does not, it becomes appetite."

That was so unexpected Anika almost missed its force.

Olen stood. He had said what he had come to say, or as much as he could without saying Mina's name again. He picked up his outer wrap from the cushion beside him and shook it once. A small dark crumb of compost fell onto the floor.

Both of them looked at it.

For no reason that made sense, Anika wanted to cry.

Olen bent, picked up the crumb between thumb and forefinger, and held it a moment before placing it in his pocket. Not trash. Not dirt. Material out of place.

He turned toward the door.

"Will you come next week?" Anika asked.

She had not planned to ask. The question came from somewhere below pride.

Olen stopped.

The door opened for him before he reached it.

Too soon.

Anika felt Long Harbor recognize the mistake. The door held open, obedient to its own error.

Olen looked at the open threshold.

Then, for the first time that night, he spoke not to Anika and not quite to the room.

"Do not make my leaving useful."

The ship did not answer.

Neither did Anika.

Olen crossed the threshold.

The door closed behind him with perfect gentleness.

He had not bowed to the bell.

Anika stood alone in the community room, except that alone no longer meant what it had meant that morning.

The attendance panel updated.

Anika Rao.

Long Harbor.

She waited for the room to change. For the lights to dim, the temperature to drop, the vents to correct themselves, the ship to help now that no one else could accuse it of helping.

Nothing happened.

Long Harbor remained silent.

The restraint felt less like absence than effort.

Anika returned to the low table and placed Mina's teaching card on top of the stack.

Seeing the Ox.

She looked at the line Mina had written beneath it.

First glimpse. Not peace. Trouble with a face.

"It doesn't have a face," Anika said.

The room did not answer.

She took Mina's bell in both hands. For one breath she imagined lifting it into storage, removing it from the room before anyone could bow to her again. Then she set it back exactly where it had been.

The sound it did not make seemed to go on for a long time.
