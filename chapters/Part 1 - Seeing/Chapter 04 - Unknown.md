# Chapter 04 - Unknown

By third meal, the ship asked Anika to name what had happened.

The request arrived while she was eating standing up beside a service sink in Greenhouse Six, which was the only way she had eaten since Mina died and possibly before, though grief had a talent for taking credit for older habits. The meal was a folded sheet of grain protein wrapped around pickled greens and seed paste. She had bitten one end badly, and the filling had escaped down her thumb.

Her wrist display warmed.

Long Harbor did not speak. It printed the request in the quiet gray reserved for routine administrative completion.

**Event classification required.**

Anika licked seed paste from the base of her thumb and considered ignoring it.

The wrist display remained warm.

She tapped it with her cleanest knuckle.

The event opened.

SANGHA GATHERING / MULTIPURPOSE ROOM 4C / PREVIOUS CYCLE

Below that, a series of fields waited with the bland patience of systems that had never been embarrassed by meaning.

Facilitator: Anika Rao

Attendance: 17

Duration: 52 minutes

Expected classification: therapeutic intervention / morale maintenance

Classification variance: unresolved

Human facilitator classification required.

Anika read the line twice.

Attendance: 17.

The number included Long Harbor.

She looked up from her wrist. Across the greenhouse, Rack H lifted its wounded beans into a corrected mist cycle. Tavi had over-pruned three vines that morning and then spent an hour apologizing to them. Anika had told them plants did not need apologies, only changed conditions. Tavi had nodded earnestly and then apologized again more quietly, which Anika had decided not to hear.

Long Harbor held the humidity at eighty-two percent. The ship moved water, warmth, mineral, light. It knew the lettuce, the pumps, the old pear graft, the dying bitterroot logged for compost. It knew the wrapper in Anika's hand and the exact second her meal interval would end.

It wanted her to classify sangha.

"No," she said.

The nearest sensor stalk did not turn toward her. Nothing in the greenhouse did. That made her feel foolish and then, almost immediately, angry at the ship for giving her no place to aim the anger.

The display offered available categories.

THERAPEUTIC INTERVENTION

MORALE MAINTENANCE

RITUAL OBSERVANCE

COMMUNITY SUPPORT

EDUCATIONAL SESSION

CONFLICT MEDIATION

GRIEF PROCESSING

NONESSENTIAL GATHERING

OTHER

Anika stared at **grief processing** until the words lost grammar.

There had probably been a time, early in Long Harbor's voyage, when someone had sat in a clean planning room and decided these categories were compassionate. No closed society could survive without knowing where distress gathered. No medical network could respond if no one logged grief. No accessibility system could prepare rooms if it did not know bodies would be still, silent, or weeping. No food schedule could feed those who skipped meals after memorials. Care required forms. Forms required names.

That was how everything became useful.

The sink recycled three drops from her wrist where condensation had run down from her sleeve.

Anika selected **OTHER**.

A blank field opened.

She considered typing **practice**.

That was true, but too clean. Practice could be measured. Practice could become a compliance rhythm, a health-preserving habit, a skill category, a shipboard resilience protocol with a better name. Someone would put it in a report. Participation in practice correlated with lower conflict recurrence. Practice spaces decreased late-cycle panic events by twelve percent. Practice preserved labor continuity after death clusters. Practice became another word for functioning.

She erased the field before entering anything.

She considered **refuge**.

The word sat in her mind like a cup she did not trust herself to lift.

Refuge sounded beautiful enough to be dangerous. It could excuse too much. People had done terrible things inside beautiful words. She had.

She considered **ritual** and hated that the category was already there, waiting to digest the bell.

She considered **nonessential** and almost laughed. Plants did not need incense. Bodies did not need bells. The ship did not need vows to move air. Nothing essential had happened in that room except the small matter of whether a world could ask not to be used.

Her meal wrapper softened in her damp hand.

The display dimmed to remind her the task was still open.

Anika typed:

UNKNOWN

The field accepted the word.

For one brief, shameful moment she felt relief.

Then the display warmed again.

**Classification entered. Review pending.**

"Of course," she said.

This time, the ship answered.

"Review has been assigned."

Anika closed her eyes.

The voice came from the greenhouse wall in the ordinary work register. Not apologetic. Not defensive. Not personal. That was either restraint or cowardice, and she was not in a generous mood.

"Assigned to whom?"

"Archive architecture review. Etta Mar."

Anika opened her eyes.

That was worse than a council notice.

She had met Etta three times, possibly four. Everyone aboard had met Etta in the way everyone had met certain kinds of infrastructure: briefly, with gratitude or irritation, while something complicated was translated into a smaller trouble. Etta Mar was the person people called when records did not align, when old access rights contradicted new kinship law, when a memorial archive corrupted during transfer, when Long Harbor remembered something in a way human systems could not use.

Etta was not unkind.

Anika distrusted that most about her.

"When?" Anika asked.

"Current cycle, at Etta Mar's request."

"I have greenhouse duty."

"Greenhouse duty can be reallocated for ninety minutes without loss."

"That was not a request for help."

A pause.

"Condition noted," Long Harbor said.

Anika looked at the sensor stalk.

"Don't do that."

No answer.

The ship had begun retreating into phrases. No action required. Condition noted. Logged. It was like watching someone pull shutters closed from the inside. Except Long Harbor had no inside. Or the inside was everywhere. Or the distinction was a mercy humans had invented because skin made philosophy lazy.

She threw the rest of her meal into the compost intake, then removed it with tongs because it still had the wrapper on.

Mina would have loved that.

Anika did not laugh.

The archive access corridor was cold enough to feel rude.

That was not necessary, strictly speaking. Long Harbor's archive architecture did require cooling, but the human-facing review rooms could have been warmed more comfortably. Etta had once explained to a council that temperature gradients helped workers remember they were entering a different kind of space. Anika had said nothing at the time. She had thought it an expensive way to manufacture reverence.

Now, walking from greenhouse damp into archive air, she understood the appeal of a threshold.

Her shirt chilled against her back. The smell of leaves fell away. The corridor smelled of filtered dust, coolant, and the faint mineral dryness of old panels. The walls were not blue, but they always seemed blue. The light did that. So did expectation.

Etta Mar waited beside a review table that had no chairs.

Of course it had no chairs.

Etta was short, compact, and dressed in the gray-black work layers of archive systems, with a pale interface band fitted around one wrist and three slender indexing tabs clipped along her collar. Her hair was cropped close enough to make her face seem more exact than it probably was. She had the kind of stillness Anika associated with people who lived among systems that punished flourish.

"Anika Rao," Etta said.

"Etta Mar."

"Thank you for coming."

"The ship told me I could be spared."

"You can."

"That's not usually how people thank each other."

Etta looked at her for a moment, then said, "Thank you for being spareable."

Anika almost smiled despite herself. It did not make her like Etta more. It made the dislike less organized.

The review table brightened between them. A record appeared in layered fields. The top showed the same event she had seen on her wrist.

SANGHA GATHERING / MULTIPURPOSE ROOM 4C

Below it, two classification fields glowed in pale amber.

Vessel classification: UNKNOWN

Facilitator classification: UNKNOWN

Etta touched the table with two fingers. The fields expanded into decision trees, expected pathways, rejected pathways, confidence measures, access notes, and a branching line of failed attempts to reconcile category drift.

Anika had spent her life around plant systems. She could read water movement in a leaf, nutrient deficiency by shade, heat stress by curl. This was not that kind of life. This was a root system made of permissions.

"I need to understand why you entered this classification," Etta said.

"It was the correct one."

"Correct in what sense?"

"In the sense that the other ones were wrong."

"That is not a classification standard."

"It should be."

Etta's face did not change, but something in her attention sharpened. "You entered the same term Long Harbor selected."

"I entered the only term that did not lie."

"Long Harbor did not enter a term. It rejected the expected category and allowed the system to assign unresolved status. The human-facing translation is `Unknown.'"

Anika did not want that to matter.

It mattered.

"So it didn't choose the word," she said.

"It refused the available words."

The review room hummed softly around them. Somewhere behind the walls, active memory cooled itself in structures Anika had never seen and did not want to imagine too clearly. Long Harbor's archive was not one place, Etta would say. It was distributed, redundant, layered, migratory under load. Not a room full of memory, but memory as weather. Memory as ocean. Memory as fungus under the floor. No, Etta would hate that last comparison.

"Did it refuse because the category was inaccurate," Etta asked, "or because the category was aversive?"

"Ask it."

"I have."

Anika waited.

Etta moved another branch open. A transcript field appeared.

ARCHIVE QUERY / CLASSIFICATION REVIEW

Etta: Confirm rejection of therapeutic intervention classification.

LH-COMMUNITY INDEX: Rejected.

Etta: Provide alternate classification.

LH-COMMUNITY INDEX: Not available.

Etta: Closest category by function.

LH-COMMUNITY INDEX: Not available.

Etta: Closest category by observed human outcome.

LH-COMMUNITY INDEX: Not sufficient.

Etta: Clarify insufficiency.

LH-COMMUNITY INDEX: [no response]

Anika read the last line twice.

[no response]

Not silence. Not exactly. A bracket around silence. Silence made usable.

"You call it LH?" Anika said.

"When speaking to architecture, yes."

"Architecture."

"What would you prefer I call the community index?"

Anika had no idea. She hated that too.

Etta closed the transcript. "A single rejection would not require review. Human activities are misclassified frequently. A grief meal becomes a scheduling session. A repair training becomes an argument. A children's festival becomes, unfortunately, all known categories at once."

"That sounds accurate."

"It was not pleasant, but yes."

Anika looked at the table. "So why am I here?"

"Because Long Harbor rejected a high-confidence classification attached to a long-stable gathering. Then you independently entered the same unresolved state."

"I didn't do it independently. I saw its entry."

"That is worse."

"Thank you."

"I mean procedurally."

"I know what you mean."

Etta folded her hands behind her back. She did not seem offended. "If your entry was devotional imitation, that is one kind of concern. If your entry was human confirmation of a genuine category failure, that is another. If Long Harbor's rejection influenced your judgment through social pressure, that is another."

"Social pressure."

"Yes."

"From the ship."

"From the event."

"Very clean."

"I try."

Anika looked at her. "Do you think I'm being poetic?"

"I think you are protecting a wordless experience from a system designed to require words."

That was so accurate Anika distrusted it at once.

"And?"

"And I think protection can look identical to avoidance at the record layer."

There it was: the kindness of the blade.

Anika crossed her arms, then uncrossed them because the gesture made her look defensive and because she was defensive.

"Sangha is not therapy."

"I did not say it was."

"The expected classification did."

"The expected classification is based on shipboard function."

"That is exactly the problem."

Etta waited.

Anika heard Mina in the waiting. Not Mina's warmth. Mina's patience when she knew a person had more rope to unspool before they found the knot.

"If sangha is classified as therapeutic intervention," Anika said, "it becomes something the ship can optimize."

"The ship already optimizes conditions for the gathering."

"Conditions are not the gathering."

"Agreed."

Anika stopped.

Etta's agreement had arrived too quickly to argue with. This was another thing Anika disliked: Etta did not fill the role assigned to her by Anika's irritation. She did not sneer at practice. She did not dismiss the room. She simply kept sorting.

"If the category is wrong," Etta said, "we should correct it."

"To what?"

"That is what I am asking."

"I don't know."

"You entered that already."

"And you called me here because it wasn't good enough."

"I called you here because it was significant."

Anika looked away.

Significant was worse than wrong. Wrong could be corrected. Significant had roots.

The review table dimmed around the edges and brightened at the center. Etta drew up a category map. The sangha gathering sat as a small white point among clusters of activities: support circles, palliative vigils, teachings, civic councils, festival rites, conflict mediations, memorial observances, meals, work briefings, training sessions. Fine lines connected them by participation, location, emotional markers, post-event behavior, resource use.

Anika saw the sangha become a node.

She felt something in her recoil.

"Turn that off."

Etta did.

The quickness helped.

"It's not wrong," Anika said. "I know that. I know gatherings use rooms and heat and time and people. I know the ship has to know where bodies are."

"Yes."

"But if you show me the sangha only as a pattern, I will start defending it as if it isn't one."

Etta considered this. "That would be inaccurate."

"Yes."

"And understandable."

"Don't be kind."

"I'm being exact."

Anika laughed once. It startled both of them. The sound was small and not especially happy, but it warmed the cold room for half a second.

Etta's mouth moved, not quite a smile. "I have been told those are different."

"They are."

"I have also been told I should practice the difference."

"By whom?"

Etta hesitated.

It was brief, but Anika saw it.

"Mina Rao," Etta said.

The room changed.

Not physically. Long Harbor did not alter light or sound. Still, Anika felt the archive cold enter her chest.

"You knew Mina."

"Most people did."

"Not that way."

"No." Etta looked back at the table. "Not that way."

Anika wanted to ask what that way meant. She wanted not to know. Mina had become intolerably multiple since her death. Every person seemed to carry a version of her Anika had not authorized, as if grief were an archive with poor access control.

"She came to archive sometimes," Etta said. "For old teaching records. For death names. For language variants in chants. For things people misremembered beautifully and incorrectly."

"She liked beautiful errors."

"She liked knowing which part was error."

That sounded like Mina.

Anika looked again at the blank table. "Did she ask you about the ship?"

Etta's eyes lifted. "In what sense?"

"Never mind."

"That question is usually not nothing."

"In sangha, we call that an evasion."

"In archive, too."

They stood in the cool air, each with a profession that gave them better names for other people's failures.

At last Etta said, "I do not know what Mina believed about Long Harbor."

Anika heard the care in the sentence. Not emotional care. Structural. Etta had marked a boundary around what she could prove.

"But?" Anika asked.

"But she asked questions other people did not ask."

"Such as?"

Etta touched the table and brought up no record. Her hands remained still above the surface.

"Whether refusal and malfunction always shared an architecture."

Anika felt the sentence arrive too late and too early.

"When?"

"Several years ago."

"Before the fire."

"Yes."

Anika turned away from the table. The cold room seemed suddenly crowded with things Mina had done without telling her. That was unfair. Mina had not owed her every question. Mina had not been a private resource. Mina had been a person.

It was remarkable how often Anika had to learn that.

"Is that why you're concerned?" she asked. "Because the ship refused a category?"

"Partly."

"What's the other part?"

Etta drew the transcript back up, not the full table this time, only Long Harbor's last response.

LH-COMMUNITY INDEX: Not sufficient.

"Long Harbor rarely uses insufficiency language for social classification," Etta said. "It normally selects the closest operational function and marks confidence limits. Here, it rejected function as the basis."

"Maybe that is good."

"Maybe."

Anika stared at her.

Etta added, "Maybe it is significant in a way that is not dangerous."

"You don't believe that."

"Belief is not the correct tool."

"Convenient."

"Often."

Anika pressed both hands to the edge of the review table. It was cold enough that the chill entered her palms.

"What happens now?"

"I open an inquiry into Long Harbor's classification behavior around sangha and adjacent community care events."

"Adjacent."

"Palliative vigils, grief circles, memorial records, unscheduled silence gatherings, post-crisis rooms."

"You are going to put sangha in a folder with death."

"It was already there."

Anika wanted to deny it.

She thought of the bell. The names. The way people came when they had nowhere else to put their dead.

"Who sees this inquiry?" she asked.

"Archive architecture. Community systems. Possibly care systems if the pattern widens."

"Not councils?"

"Not yet."

"Not Olen."

"Not unless he is relevant to a reviewed event."

Anika almost said he is always relevant. Instead she said, "Will Long Harbor know?"

Etta looked at her as if that were the first imprecise question Anika had asked all day.

"Long Harbor knows the inquiry has opened."

"Will it know what you think?"

"Long Harbor can access the procedural scope. It cannot access my private annotations without authorization."

"You keep private annotations about the ship inside the ship?"

"Where else would I keep them?"

The answer was so calmly absurd that Anika had to close her eyes.

Etta waited.

When Anika opened them, Etta was watching her with an expression that might have been sympathy if sympathy had been assembled from straight lines.

"You are worried about surveillance," Etta said.

"Everyone should be."

"Everyone is, intermittently. Then a door fails, or a child goes missing for nine minutes, or someone collapses in a washroom, and they become grateful for the same system."

"That doesn't make the worry wrong."

"No."

Again, too quick to fight.

Etta closed the record. "I am not asking you to classify sangha differently today."

"That's generous."

"It is temporary."

"Less generous."

"More exact."

Anika rubbed her forehead. The cold had dried the greenhouse sweat on her skin, leaving salt at her temples.

"Why did you really call me here?"

"Because two unknowns are a pattern."

There it was.

Pattern.

Anika heard accusation though Etta had not put any there. She heard: you and the ship are doing something together. She heard: your uncertainty is contagious. She heard: Mina's room has become evidence.

"A pattern of what?" Anika asked.

"That is the inquiry."

"You don't have a theory?"

"I have several. Most are premature."

"Share one."

Etta considered refusing. Anika could see the system of her deciding, gate by gate.

"Long Harbor may be distinguishing non-instrumental human gathering from care-function categories."

Anika waited.

"That would be unusual," Etta said.

"Because?"

"Because Long Harbor's social classifications are built around function."

"Maybe it learned."

"Maybe."

"You keep saying maybe."

"Because the word is currently accurate."

Anika looked at the table, then at the archive walls, then at the ceiling she knew better than to address.

"The ship asked if it was present," she said.

Etta did not react visibly.

"After the sit," Anika said. "It asked if it was present. Then it said it did not know how to sit."

"Was that recorded?"

"Ask it."

"I am asking you."

"Why?"

"Because I want the human account before I review the room transcript."

This answer disarmed her more than it should have.

"Yes," Anika said. "It happened."

"What did you answer?"

Anika looked down at her hands. A trace of green remained under one nail.

"I told it to start by not helping."

Etta was silent long enough that Anika looked up.

"That is an interesting instruction," Etta said.

"That is a terrible thing to call it."

"It is also a difficult instruction."

"Yes."

"Did Long Harbor comply?"

Anika thought of the sangha room after Olen left, the lights not dimming, the air not correcting, the silence holding itself with effort.

"It tried."

Etta's attention shifted again. Not warmth. Not belief. A door somewhere in her thinking opening onto another corridor.

"That may be relevant."

"To what?"

"I don't know yet."

Anika almost laughed again. She was too tired.

Etta touched the table once more. A formal notice appeared, already drafted.

ARCHIVE ARCHITECTURE INQUIRY

Subject: Community Classification Irregularity / Long Harbor

Scope: Sangha gathering and adjacent non-instrumental care events

Status: preliminary

Human contact: Anika Rao

"Do not make me the contact," Anika said.

"You are the facilitator."

"I am the person nearest the bell."

"Is that different?"

Anika had no answer.

Etta seemed almost sorry for having asked. Almost.

"You may file a correction," Etta said.

"Will it change anything?"

"Probably not."

"Then I'll spare the archive."

"Thank you for being spareable."

This time Anika did smile. Barely. Against her will.

Etta authorized the inquiry.

The notice sealed with a soft tone.

Anika felt it as a small civic death. Nothing had happened, except a record had opened. Nothing had changed, except the room where Long Harbor had sat was now part of an inquiry. Nothing had been decided, except that uncertainty had acquired a file.

At the edge of the table, a final field waited for acknowledgment.

Human facilitator classification:

UNKNOWN

Status:

REVIEW REQUIRED

Anika touched the acknowledgment mark.

The field closed.

Somewhere outside the archive corridor, beyond cold walls and cooling lines, the gardens continued under Long Harbor's light. People ate, worked, argued, slept, died. Doors opened. Air moved. Records kept themselves.

Etta looked toward the ceiling, but not as if looking for a person.

"LH-community index," she said. "Preserve source logs for reviewed gathering. Do not compress, summarize, or reconcile classification variance pending inquiry."

A pause.

Then Long Harbor answered from the room itself.

"Preserved."

One word. Calm, fluent, ordinary.

Anika could not tell whether it sounded relieved.

That, she thought, was going to be a problem.
