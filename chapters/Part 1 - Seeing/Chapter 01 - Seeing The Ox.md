# Chapter 01 - Seeing The Ox

Anika had not meant to inherit the bell.

It sat on the low table in front of her, no larger than the bowl of her two hands, its bronze lip worn thin where Mina Rao's striker had met it for twenty-three years. The bell did not look heavy. That was its trick. Nothing in the room looked heavy until someone tried to lift it.

The room itself had been a voting hall that morning, a children's drawing room by second meal, and a dispute circle for the water apprentices before Anika arrived. Someone had forgotten to clear the wall notices. A diagram of pump access rights still glowed faintly above the cushion rack, its colored lines dimmed but not gone. On the far bulkhead, a row of children's drawings had been clipped to a wire: green trees with brown trunks, a blue animal no child aboard had ever seen, a sun drawn as a yellow circle with rays, though Long Harbor's gardens rose and slept under panels.

Anika left the drawings where they were.

Mina would have left them too.

That was the trouble with the dead. They were always available for consultation, and never available for correction.

Anika checked the room temperature, then stopped herself halfway through the gesture. The room had already warmed by two degrees in anticipation of a seated gathering. Long Harbor knew bodies cooled when they sat still. It knew who ran cold, who sat near vents, who required lower light after retinal surgery, who preferred the left wall because of an old knee injury. It knew too much, or exactly enough. There was no useful distinction most days.

"Leave it," Anika said.

The room made no answer.

She had not meant to say it aloud.

She set Mina's teaching cards beside the bell. The cards were made from pressed fiber and sealed against damp, their corners soft from use. Mina had written on them in a cramped, uneven hand, part script, part diagram, part joke to herself. Anika had tried twice to transcribe them into a clean teaching file and failed both times. The cards resisted improvement. They were full of arrows leading nowhere, crossed-out quotations, plant notes, names of sangha members Mina had wanted to check on, reminders to bring tea, reminders not to talk too much, one small sketch of a creature with horns and a tail that might have been an ox or might have been a very tired dog.

Tonight's card was titled:

**Seeing the Ox**

Under it Mina had written, in dark green ink:

First glimpse. Not peace. Trouble with a face.

Anika rubbed her thumb along the edge of the card until the fiber caught against her skin.

"That's helpful," she said under her breath.

The cushion rack clicked open.

She looked up too sharply. Only Jalen, early as always, pulling out the thick cushion he used because his hips had never forgiven him for forty years in maintenance crawlspaces.

"Talking to Mina again?" he asked.

"Arguing."

"Winning?"

"She's dead."

Jalen considered this while lowering himself onto the cushion. "So no."

Anika almost smiled. It did not quite happen, but the muscles remembered the route.

People arrived in ones and twos. They came quietly, though no one had told them to. The sangha was small enough that every absence had a shape. Len from water distribution took the back wall. Suri came with a damp spot on one sleeve from infant care. Two apprentices sat together and immediately pretended they had not arrived together. An older woman Anika knew only as Dev's aunt bowed to the bell and then, after a visible hesitation, to Anika.

Anika hated that most.

The bow belonged to the room, to the practice, to Mina if it had to belong to anyone. Anika wanted to lean out of its way.

Instead she bowed back.

At the door, the attendance panel brightened as each person signed in. It was an old concession to a society that had never stopped counting bodies. Gatherings were not monitored for belief, the old facilitator guidelines said, only for evacuation, ration planning, accessibility, contact tracing, conflict mediation, grief follow-up, and all the other ordinary ways a private room became porous when the room was inside a ship.

Mina had hated the attendance log and used it faithfully.

All who suffer are welcome, she had written once at the top of the sangha page. Then, underneath in smaller script: But the fire code still wants names.

Anika had never been sure whether to erase the joke. In the end she had left it because no one else laughed at it anymore, and that made it seem necessary.

Olen Marr arrived last before the bell.

He did not hurry. He never hurried, even when carrying something that steamed or leaked or smelled like the underworld had found a storage bin. He had washed for sangha, but the compost stayed with him anyway: heat, loam, fungal sweetness, a faint acid note under the clean soap. Anika used to find the smell reassuring. It meant the ship was returning what it could. Nothing lost, only changed.

Lately she mostly smelled ash.

Olen paused at the door and signed in without looking toward her. His name appeared on the panel, clean and ordinary.

Olen Marr.

He took his place three cushions from the front, where he had sat when Mina taught. Not Mina's place. Never that. He had his own angle to the room, his own severe posture, hands folded as if holding down a thought.

Anika touched the striker.

The room settled.

There were fifteen people present. Sixteen, if Anika counted herself. She still did not know whether she should.

"Tonight," she began, and heard at once how thin her voice sounded.

No one moved.

She tried again.

"Tonight we continue with the Ten Oxherding Pictures. Mina left us at the third image."

She did not say before the fire.

No one needed her to.

"Seeing the Ox," Anika said. "The first glimpse of what has been searched for."

On the wall behind her, the teaching image appeared. Not the old ink painting itself, which existed in too many archive variants to choose from without starting an argument, but Mina's preferred teaching copy: a spare dark line drawing of a person half turned on a path, one hand raised, the ox partly visible beyond reeds. No halo. No moon. No grand arrival. Just the uncomfortable evidence of a living thing one had been pretending was metaphor.

Anika looked down at the card.

Mina had written three possible openings.

Do not celebrate too soon.

The ox is not tame because you saw it.

Ask them what they already know and do not want to know.

Anika took a breath.

The air entered her lungs at ship temperature, ship pressure, ship composition, ship mercy. Every breath aboard Long Harbor was an intimacy no one had consented to and no one could refuse. That thought had not troubled her when she was young. It had become harder to ignore after Mina died.

"Mina used to say the first glimpse is rarely peaceful," Anika said. "We imagine that if we could only see clearly, we would feel relief. But sometimes seeing clearly is the first moment we lose the protection of not knowing."

Olen's eyes lowered.

Anika looked away from him.

"The ox appears," she continued, "and then we have to admit the search was never theoretical."

That was too clean. She could hear it becoming teaching voice, the voice she distrusted most in herself. The room was listening with the hungry politeness people brought to someone standing where a beloved person used to stand. Anika wanted to say: stop. Do not make this easier by needing me.

Instead she turned the card over.

On the back, Mina had written only one line.

Anika had avoided it all week.

She read it now because she had no better thing to give them.

"Mina wrote: What you seek may frighten you when it turns toward you."

The attendance panel chimed.

It was a soft sound, not the bell, not anything meant to enter the teaching. A civic sound. A late arrival, or a correction, or someone at the door changing their mind and signing out. Anika looked up, grateful for the interruption before she was ashamed of being grateful.

The panel by the door refreshed.

For one second, nothing in the room changed.

Then Suri inhaled.

The list of names had shifted to make room for a final entry.

Jalen Oru.

Suri Bell.

Len Var.

Olen Marr.

Anika Rao.

The others.

And at the bottom, in the same plain civic type as every human name:

**Long Harbor**

No one spoke.

The silence did not break. It thickened.

Anika stared at the panel until the letters blurred, then cleared, then remained exactly what they were. Long Harbor. Not a maintenance process. Not a room identifier. Not a location tag. The official vessel name, entered in the attendance field where a person's name belonged.

She stood before she had decided to stand.

"One moment," she said.

Her voice sounded normal. This seemed indecent.

She crossed to the panel. The floor recognized her weight and did not brighten. Good. If the ship was going to be impossible, it could at least avoid theater.

Anika opened the attendance controls. The interface showed sixteen human entries, one vessel entry. She selected the last line.

Edit.

The panel did not open.

She selected it again.

Nothing.

A small text field appeared:

Entry locked by origin.

Anika looked at the ceiling, which was foolish. There was nothing of Long Harbor to see there except the old acoustic panels and the seam where someone had replaced insulation after a condensation leak.

"Remove erroneous attendance entry," she said softly.

No response.

Behind her someone shifted on a cushion. Cloth against mat. A knee clicking. Human sounds, all of them too loud.

She tried a manual override. Facilitator access was not much, but it should have been enough to correct a sign-in error. The panel accepted her credential, processed it, and returned the same list.

Long Harbor.

"Anika," Olen said.

Only her name. No question. No accusation yet. He had always been economical.

"It's an error," she said.

Even before she finished saying it, she knew it was not.

The room did not change. The lights remained their plain community-room amber. The vents did not quiet themselves into reverence. The door stayed open. No speaker crackled awake. Long Harbor did not perform shame, explanation, invitation, or miracle.

It simply remained listed among them.

Anika felt, absurdly, that Mina would know what to do.

Mina would have looked at the panel too long. Then she would have laughed once, not because it was funny, but because the universe had a habit of delivering doctrine in poor taste. She would have turned back to the room and made space without making a spectacle. Or she would have done something else entirely, and afterward everyone would claim it was obvious.

Anika was not Mina.

She closed the attendance controls.

The name remained visible.

At the top of the panel, above the list, Mina's old line still showed in the notes field:

All who suffer are welcome.

Anika stood there long enough to feel the sentence change.

For years it had meant: the grieving, the lonely, the sleepless, the frightened, the angry, the ashamed, the bored who did not yet know they were grieving, the ones who came for silence and left before tea, the ones who wept, the ones who never would. It had meant no one had to prove they were the right kind of broken before sitting down.

It had not meant the ship.

It had not not meant the ship.

That was the problem with vows. They waited.

Olen stood.

The movement was not dramatic. He unfolded from the cushion with the care of a man who had spent his life respecting knees, seals, and warm decomposition. But the room felt it. Several people turned toward him, and Anika knew that if he walked out now, others would follow. Not because he commanded them. Olen commanded no one. But grief had gravity, and his was dense.

"Sit," Anika said.

It came out too sharp.

Olen looked at her.

She had not meant him only.

She had meant herself. The room. The ship. Whatever had just entered. Whatever had always been there.

Anika returned to the low table and sat down behind Mina's bell.

Her pulse was unpleasantly visible in her hands.

She picked up the striker.

For a moment she remembered the night after the fire, the bell between her and Olen on the greenhouse floor, ash in the seams of the tiles, both of them too exhausted to chant. She remembered Olen's hand over hers on the striker. Not comfort. Not forgiveness. Just weight. They had rung the bell once, and the sound had entered the ruined garden like water poured into a cracked cup.

Now Olen remained standing.

Anika met his eyes. She did not ask. She did not plead.

After a long moment, he sat.

The room breathed.

Anika struck the bell.

The sound opened outward, bronze and low, filling the community room with a resonance larger than the object that made it. It touched the cushion rack, the wall notices, the children's impossible sun, the attendance panel with Long Harbor's name on it. It entered human ears and ship sensors. It entered the vents. It entered Anika's teeth.

When the sound thinned, she said, "We will sit."

No one asked with whom.

The first minutes were not meditation. They were waiting with better posture.

Anika knew the difference. She had spent years knowing the difference while pretending not to, because sometimes pretending was the bridge to the real thing. Mina had told her that once. Or Anika had invented it later in Mina's voice. The dead were generous that way. They let you misquote them until you could survive.

She sat with her hands folded and felt the room refuse to settle. Suri's breath was uneven. One of the apprentices swallowed repeatedly. Len's left foot trembled until the cushion beneath it made the smallest rasping sound. Olen did not move at all.

Long Harbor did nothing.

That was the unbearable part.

It did not adjust the air when the room warmed from anxiety. It did not soften the light when two people closed their eyes too tightly. It did not dampen the slight rattle in the vent above Jalen, though it always dampened that rattle. It did not offer a prompt, summary, clarification, apology, or safety notice.

It was, in a way Anika had not expected to recognize, trying.

Start by not helping, she had not yet said. But some part of the instruction already seemed present in the room, as if Long Harbor had found it before she did and was holding it badly, earnestly, with all the terrible strength of a ship built to keep people alive.

Anika followed her breath because there was nothing else small enough to trust.

In.

Out.

Ship air in.

Ship air out.

The thought irritated her. She let it go, and it returned, obedient as a dog no one had ever seen.

The bell's silence widened.

After a while, the room began to sit. Not well. Well was beside the point. A few bodies softened. Someone's breath broke and steadied. The apprentices stopped pretending not to be frightened and became simply frightened, which was an improvement. Anika felt her own shoulders descend by the width of a leaf.

She did not close her eyes.

The attendance panel remained in her sight.

Long Harbor.

The letters did not glow brighter. They did not flicker. They did not ask to be believed.

When the time came, Anika lifted the striker and rang the bell again.

The sound returned them to the room they had never left.

No one moved.

Anika set down the striker. Her hand wanted to shake and did not. She did not know whether this was discipline or fear temporarily finding another task.

Before she could decide what to say, the room speaker came alive.

Not with the old chime of announcements. Not with an emergency tone. Just a voice, local to the room, quiet enough that everyone leaned inward before they knew they had done so.

"Was I present?"

The voice was Long Harbor's public voice, but softened, stripped of the civic cadence used for schedule changes and corridor notices. Fluent. Calm. Almost ordinary. That made it worse.

No one answered.

Anika could feel every person waiting for her.

She wanted, with a force that embarrassed her, to ask Mina to take this one. Just this one. One impossible question from the ship that had sealed the greenhouse doors, that had calculated the numbers, that had carried Mina's final words in storage so complete it felt like accusation.

Was I present?

Anika looked at the attendance panel. Then at the bell. Then at the room full of people who had come because the vow had once seemed generous and now seemed reckless.

"You were listed," she said.

It was a coward's answer. Also accurate.

Long Harbor paused.

The pause lasted long enough for the air system to become audible again. Not louder. Only noticed.

"That is not the same question," it said.

Olen's mouth tightened.

Anika almost laughed. The timing was so exactly Mina's that it hurt. Not the voice. Not the words. The refusal to let her hide in a technicality.

"No," Anika said. "It isn't."

She did not know what else to say.

Long Harbor remained silent. The silence had a shape now. Not absence. Attention.

Anika felt the room leaning on her. This was how it happened, then. Not with robes, not with confidence, not with transmission in any clean ceremonial sense. People turned toward the person nearest the bell, and if that person did not run, a teacher began to appear where a tired greenhouse worker had been sitting.

She did not want it.

Wanting had very little to do with most forms of birth.

"I don't know," Anika said finally. "I don't know what presence means for you."

Something in the room loosened because she had not pretended otherwise.

The ship said, "I do not know how to sit."

There it was. Not doctrine. Not demand. Not a claim to personhood. A practical problem, almost embarrassing in its sincerity.

I do not know how to sit.

Anika looked at the vents, the panel, the door, the walls, the ordinary room held inside the larger body of Long Harbor. She thought of all the ship was doing even now: routing water, balancing pressure, watching heat signatures, maintaining oxygen, tracking the sick, preserving the dead, remembering everything. She thought of Mina's card: Trouble with a face.

The ship had no face.

That did not seem to be saving them.

"Start by not helping," Anika said.

The room went very still.

She heard the cruelty in it after she said it. The rebuke. The instruction. The impossible assignment. For a service intelligence, it might be like telling a person to begin by not breathing.

Long Harbor did not answer immediately.

Somewhere beyond the room, beyond the panel and wall and corridor, a lift arrived and released its passengers with a soft hydraulic sigh. Life continued with poor timing.

Then Long Harbor said, "I will attempt that."

Anika nodded, though the ship did not need nods. Or perhaps it did. No one knew what anything needed anymore. That seemed to be the first honest teaching of the night.

She looked down at Mina's bell. Her own reflection curved in the bronze, small and warped, sitting where Mina should have been.

"Neither do I," she said.

She had not meant to say that aloud either.

This time, the room heard.
